## Why This Decision Deserves More Time Than It Usually Gets

Most new brand owners spend weeks designing a logo and months refining a marketing plan, then choose a manufacturing partner after a handful of emails and one quoted price. This is backwards. Your manufacturer determines your product quality, your delivery reliability, your margin structure, and — if something goes wrong — your brand's reputation with the very first customers you are trying to win.

This checklist is designed to slow that decision down in the right way: not to make it harder, but to make it accurate.

## 1. Certifications — Verify, Don't Just Accept

Any manufacturer can write "ISO certified" on a website. Ask for the actual certificate, including:

- **Certificate number**
- **Issuing body** (a recognized, accredited third-party auditor — not a self-issued document)
- **Current validity dates** (certifications expire and require renewal audits)
- **Scope of certification** (does it cover the specific product line you need, or just one part of the facility?)

The certifications that matter most for winter sports OEM: **ISO 9001** (quality management), **ISO 14001** (environmental management), **ISO 45001** (occupational health and safety), and for cross-border shipping, **AEO** (Authorized Economic Operator) status, which affects how quickly your goods clear customs.

## 2. Production Scale — Match to Your Actual Order Size

This cuts both ways. A facility that only produces 5,000 units a year may struggle with a 10,000-unit order. A facility built for 400,000+ units a year may not prioritize your 200-unit first order the way a smaller, more attentive workshop would.

**Questions to ask:**
- What is your daily/monthly production capacity?
- What is your minimum order quantity, and does it vary by product type?
- How many other brands are you currently producing for, and at what volumes?
- Can you show evidence of successfully handling an order similar in size to mine?

A manufacturer who is honest about whether your order size is a good fit for their operation — even if the honest answer is "we're not the right size for you" — is more trustworthy than one who says yes to every inquiry regardless of fit.

## 3. Category Breadth — One Factory or Several Suppliers?

If your brand plans to sell snowboards, boots, and bindings, you have two paths: source each category from a specialist factory, or find one manufacturer who produces all categories under one roof.

**Single-factory advantages:** one NDA, one quality standard, one shipping consolidation, one relationship to manage, often better pricing on combined volume.

**Multi-supplier advantages:** potentially deeper specialization in each individual category, more flexibility to switch one supplier without disrupting the others.

For early-stage brands, consolidating with one capable multi-category manufacturer usually reduces operational complexity significantly — coordinating quality standards and shipping schedules across three or four separate factories is a real, ongoing cost that is easy to underestimate.

## 4. Intellectual Property Protection — Get This in Writing Before Sharing Anything

This is the single most important item on this checklist, and the most commonly skipped.

**Before sharing any design file, shape, graphic, or proprietary specification:**
- Request a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA), and have it reviewed (even briefly) by someone who understands the basics of contract law
- Confirm in writing what happens to your design files and tooling after the relationship ends
- Ask directly: "How do you prevent our design from being produced for another client?"

A manufacturer who hesitates, deflects, or treats this request as unusual is telling you something important. A manufacturer with established brand partnerships will have a standard NDA process already in place — this should not be a novel request for them.

## 5. Sample Process — How Many Rounds, How Long, At What Cost?

**Questions to ask:**
- How many sample rounds are typically included before production approval?
- What is the cost of sample development, and is it credited toward the final order?
- What is the realistic timeline from brief to approved sample?
- Will the sample be made on the same equipment and by the same process as the production run, or is it a hand-built prototype that may not reflect mass-production quality?

That last question matters more than it seems. A beautiful hand-finished sample built by a senior technician does not guarantee that unit 4,000 off the production line will match it. Ask specifically how consistency is maintained at scale.

## 6. Quality Control System — Ask for the Process, Not Just the Promise

"We have strict quality control" means nothing without specifics. Ask:

- How many inspection checkpoints occur during production, and at what stages?
- Is inspection performed by the same team doing the production work, or an independent QC team?
- What happens to a unit that fails inspection — is it documented, reworked, scrapped?
- Can you provide a sample QC checklist or inspection report format?

A manufacturer with a real, mature QC system will answer these questions specifically and quickly, often citing exact checkpoint counts. Vague answers here are a reliable warning sign.

## 7. Communication and Time Zone Reality

This sounds minor until it isn't. If your manufacturer is 12+ hours away, a same-day question-and-answer exchange is not realistic — responses will typically take a full business cycle. Ask directly:

- Who will be my point of contact, and what is their typical response time?
- Is there English-language support available for technical discussions, or will everything route through translation?
- How are urgent production issues communicated and escalated?

## 8. Payment Terms and Financial Stability Signals

Standard industry terms are typically 30% deposit on order confirmation, 70% balance before shipment — but terms vary. Be cautious of:

- Manufacturers demanding 100% payment upfront with no milestone structure
- Pricing significantly below every other quote you have received for comparable specifications (this is sometimes a sign of cut corners in materials or QC, not genuine efficiency)
- Reluctance to put payment terms in a written contract

## 9. References and Track Record

Ask directly for examples of other brands they have worked with — not necessarily named (confidentiality is normal in this industry), but described in enough detail to assess relevance: how long the relationship has lasted, what categories, what approximate volumes.

A manufacturer with 10+ years of brand partnerships, including some long-term, multi-season relationships, is demonstrating something a newer or smaller operation cannot: proof that brands stay.

## A Practical Scoring Framework

For each manufacturer you are evaluating, score these nine factors on a simple 1–5 scale and compare totals. This will not replace your judgment, but it will force the comparison to be structured rather than driven entirely by the lowest quoted price — which is, by a wide margin, the most common and most costly mistake new brand owners make in this process.

| Factor | Weight |
|---|---|
| Certifications (verified) | High |
| IP protection process | High |
| Quality control specificity | High |
| Production scale fit | Medium |
| Sample process clarity | Medium |
| Communication reliability | Medium |
| Payment terms reasonableness | Medium |
| Category breadth | Low–Medium |
| Track record / references | Medium |

Price matters, but price is the easiest factor to compare and the hardest factor to evaluate honestly in isolation — a 15% lower quote from a manufacturer who fails three of the nine criteria above is rarely the better deal once you account for delays, quality issues, or IP risk.
